Overnight Developments

watch TSMC reportedly negotiating 5-10% base price hikes for 2027, plus new HPC-overage surcharge

Nikkei Asia reports (anonymous sources, not confirmed by TSMC) that TSMC is negotiating base price increases of 5-10% for 2027, applying to both mature nodes (12nm/16nm/28nm) and advanced nodes (N3/N2) — broader than the AI/HPC-only framing in earlier headlines. Separately, and more novel: orders exceeding previously agreed HPC/AI volumes would carry an additional 10-15% surcharge, meaning some datacenter customers could see combined increases north of 20%. TSMC's spokesperson response was a non-denial: "our pricing strategy is strategic, not opportunistic." CEO C.C. Wei said in June he wants to raise prices but avoid the abrupt hikes "some memory firms" have imposed — directionally consistent, not a confirmation of these specific figures.

This is incremental to, not a restatement of, the already-known 15% N3 2H26 hike (TrendForce, 5/27) — that report already flagged a 5-10% follow-on for 3nm in 2027; what's new here is the extension to mature nodes and the overage-surcharge mechanism. Relevant to P010/P011/P031 (TSMC revenue growth and gross-margin thresholds) but not a probability trigger on its own given the sourcing quality — flag as a lead to watch for confirmation at the Q3 call.

TSM ADR +3.3-3.8% premarket (~$416-418 vs. $402.30 prior close); TSMC (2330.TW) closed Taipei +3.88% at 2,410 TWD.

watch Google reportedly developing "Frozen" chip — motivated by an internal compute shortage

The Information (exclusive, corroborated by CNBC/TechCrunch, 7/20): Google is developing a chip codenamed "Frozen" that hardwires portions of the Gemini architecture directly into silicon — distinct from the existing TPU line, not a new TPU generation. Claimed 6-10x perf/watt vs. current Google silicon; target launch ~2028; locks in only if Gemini's underlying architecture doesn't change before then (real obsolescence risk). The notable detail: reporting frames this as driven by an internal compute shortage severe enough that Google Cloud is reportedly turning away external business — a demand-outstripping-supply signal worth logging against the computational-abundance thesis, though it's a single unconfirmed report and could reflect internal capacity-allocation choices rather than a hard compute ceiling.

Source: The Information, CNBC, 7/20.
